Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about traveling in China

1. Do I need a visa to travel to China?

Most foreign nationals require a visa to enter China. The standard tourist visa (L) is valid for 30 days single entry and typically costs $140-200 depending on nationality. Since 2024, China has significantly expanded visa-free transit: citizens of 54 countries can now enjoy 144-hour (6-day) visa-free stays at major international airports in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Chengdu, and other gateway cities—allowing exploration of the city region without a visa if you hold an onward ticket to a third country. Additionally, several European and Asian countries now have full visa-free access for stays up to 15-30 days. Policies change frequently—check the latest rules with your local Chinese embassy before booking.

2. What is the best time to visit China?

Spring (April to May) and autumn (September to October) deliver the most pleasant weather across most of China—clear skies, comfortable temperatures (15-25°C), and manageable tourist crowds. Spring brings cherry blossoms in Wuhan and Kunming, while autumn paints Beijing's parks in gold and red. Summer (June-August) brings intense heat and humidity to the south and east, but is ideal for exploring northern regions like Inner Mongolia and Harbin. Avoid Chinese New Year (late January/February—everything closes down) and the first week of October (National Day Golden Week—attractions become overwhelmingly crowded with domestic tourists). Check our individual city guides for month-by-month weather and crowd forecasts.

3. Is it safe to travel in China?

China is consistently rated among the safest travel destinations globally, with extremely low rates of violent crime against tourists. Major cities like Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou are safe to walk in at any hour, including late at night. The primary safety concerns are practical, not violent: petty theft in heavily crowded tourist areas (especially around train stations and popular attractions), taxi scams (always use official taxis with meters or ride-hailing apps like Didi), and traffic safety when crossing roads (driving patterns differ from Western norms and jaywalking is common). Solo female travelers consistently report feeling very safe in China. Pickpocketing is the most common issue in tourist-dense areas—keep your phone and wallet in front pockets or a secured bag.

4. How do I get internet access in China?

China operates the Great Firewall, which blocks Google, Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, WhatsApp, YouTube, and most Western social media platforms. The essential preparation: purchase and install a reliable VPN on all your devices BEFORE arriving in China—most VPN websites are blocked inside the country. Test the VPN thoroughly before your flight. Once in China, local SIM cards are inexpensive and widely available at airports and convenience stores. China Mobile and China Unicom offer tourist-friendly packages with generous data allowances. Wi-Fi is ubiquitous in hotels, cafes, and restaurants, but the firewall applies to all connections. Download and configure WeChat and Alipay before arrival—they are essential for daily life including payments, messaging, ride-hailing, and booking attractions. Google Maps does not work in China; download Baidu Maps or Amap (高德地图) for navigation.

5. How do I get around Chinese cities?

China has arguably the world's most advanced public transportation infrastructure. High-speed trains connect all major cities at speeds up to 350 km/h—the Beijing-Shanghai route covers 1,318 km in just 4.5 hours. Within cities, metro systems are extensive, clean, and affordable: Beijing (27 lines, fares 3-9 RMB), Shanghai (20 lines), Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Chengdu all operate world-class subway networks. Ride-hailing via Didi Chuxing (China's Uber, available in English) is reliable and inexpensive by Western standards—a typical 15-minute ride costs 15-30 RMB ($2-4). Traditional taxis are widely available but drivers rarely speak English—always have your destination written in Chinese characters to show the driver. City public buses are comprehensive but challenging for non-Chinese speakers. Shared bikes (Mobike, Hellobike) are ubiquitous and cost just 1-2 RMB per ride.

6. Do I need to speak Chinese to travel in China?

While not strictly essential in major tourist cities, knowing even basic survival phrases dramatically improves your experience and the warmth of your reception. In Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou, major tourist attractions, international hotel chains, and upscale restaurants offer English support. However, outside these bubbles—in local neighborhoods, smaller restaurants, traditional markets, and especially in second and third-tier cities—English is rare. Essential preparation: download an offline translation app like Pleco or Google Translate with the Chinese language pack downloaded before your trip. Learn five key phrases: 'ni hao' (hello), 'xie xie' (thank you), 'duo shao qian' (how much does it cost), 'wo yao zhe ge' (I want this one), and 'cesuo zai nar' (where is the bathroom). Always carry your hotel's business card to show taxi drivers. Patience and a smile bridge most language barriers.

7. How much does it cost to travel in China?

China offers exceptional value across all budget levels. Budget travelers can manage comfortably on $30-50 per day: hostel dorm beds ($8-15/night), street food and local restaurants ($2-5 per meal), and public transportation. Mid-range travel with comfortable 3-4 star hotels ($40-80/night), restaurant meals ($8-15 per meal), and occasional domestic flights runs $80-150 per day. Luxury travel with five-star hotels ($120-300/night), private guides ($50-100/day), and fine dining can run $200-500+ per day. The biggest expenses are international flights and visa fees. Domestic high-speed train tickets typically cost $20-100 per trip depending on distance. Street food is astonishingly affordable—a full meal of dumplings or noodles costs $2-5. Mid-range restaurant dinners with several dishes run $10-25 per person. Major attraction entry tickets range from $5-30 (the Forbidden City is 60 RMB, about $8).

8. Which Chinese cities should I visit on my first trip?

For a classic first-time China itinerary, the golden triangle of Beijing, Xi'an, and Shanghai forms the ideal foundation: Beijing (3-4 days)—the Great Wall at Mutianyu, Forbidden City, Temple of Heaven, Summer Palace, and exploring traditional hutong alleyways. Xi'an (2-3 days)—the Terracotta Warriors, ancient City Wall (bike the full 14km circuit), Muslim Quarter street food, and the Big Wild Goose Pagoda. Shanghai (2-3 days)—the Bund waterfront with its colonial-era skyline contrasting Pudong's futuristic towers, French Concession tree-lined streets, Yu Garden, and Nanjing Road. With more time, add: Chengdu (2-3 days) for giant pandas at the breeding center and life-changing Sichuan cuisine; Guilin and Yangshuo (2-3 days) for the iconic karst mountain landscapes and Li River bamboo rafting; and Hangzhou (1-2 days) for West Lake, tea plantations, and the serenity that inspired Chinese poetry for centuries. Each of these cities has detailed guides with itineraries in our directory.

9. How do I pay for things in China?

China is the world's most cashless society. WeChat Pay and Alipay together account for over 90% of all transactions—from street food vendors to luxury malls. For foreign travelers, Alipay now supports international credit card linking: download Alipay, verify your identity with your passport, and link your Visa/Mastercard. You can then scan QR codes everywhere. Cash is still accepted at most establishments, though some small vendors may struggle with change. International credit cards work at major hotels and high-end restaurants but are rarely accepted elsewhere. Set up mobile payment tools before your trip for the smoothest experience.

10. What should I pack for a trip to China?

Your essential China packing list: the most comfortable walking shoes you own (you'll average 10-20,000 steps daily sightseeing), a reliable VPN installed and tested before departure, a high-capacity portable battery pack (10,000+ mAh), small packets of tissues (public restrooms rarely provide toilet paper), hand sanitizer, any prescription medications in original packaging with a doctor's note, photocopies of your passport and visa stored separately from originals, and a universal travel adapter (China uses Type A/C/I sockets at 220V). If visiting temples or religious sites, pack clothing that covers shoulders and knees. Pre-download essential apps: Alipay (linked to your international card), Didi (ride-hailing), and a translation app with offline Chinese support. Most importantly, bring an open mind—China will surprise you.
