Nanjing

The Ancient Capital of Six Dynasties — Where History Breathes

Core Scenic Spots

Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum (Zhongshan Ling)

Location: Southern slope of Purple Mountain (Zijin Shan), Xuanwu District

Historical Background: Built between 1926 and 1929, Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum is the final resting place of Dr. Sun Yat-sen, the founding father of modern China. The mausoleum ascends through 392 stone steps, flanked by ancient pines and cypresses, creating an atmosphere of solemn grandeur. The architecture blends traditional Chinese palace design with modern memorial aesthetics — a masterpiece that embodies the spirit of a nation in transition.

Highlights:

  • Climb the 392 steps — each tier reveals a more magnificent view of the mausoleum and the purple mountains beyond
  • The Memorial Hall houses a solemn marble statue of Dr. Sun Yat-sen
  • Overlook the entire Nanjing city skyline from the top platform
  • Adjacent to Linggu Temple and Ming Xiaoling — form the "Three Great Sites of Purple Mountain"
  • Surrounding plum blossom grove bursts into bloom every February-March

Opening Hours: 8:30 AM – 5:00 PM (closed Mondays for memorial hall maintenance)

Tickets: Free (reservation required via WeChat mini-program)

Transportation: Metro Line 2 to Muxuyuan Station (Exit 1), then 15-minute walk or shuttle bus (¥10)

Best Duration: 2–3 hours

Best Season: Spring (March–April plum blossoms) and autumn (October–November golden foliage)

Tips: Wear comfortable shoes — it's a real climb. Arrive before 9 AM to avoid crowds. The view from the top at sunrise is breathtaking.

Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum

Location: Southern foot of Purple Mountain, Xuanwu District

Historical Background: The tomb of Zhu Yuanzhang, the founding emperor of the Ming Dynasty (1368–1398), Ming Xiaoling is the largest imperial mausoleum in China and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Sacred Way (Shendao) stretches over 800 meters, lined with 12 pairs of stone animals and civil/military officials — a silent stone army guarding the emperor for 600 years. Its architectural layout influenced the design of the Ming Tombs in Beijing and royal tombs across East Asia.

Highlights:

  • Walk the Sacred Way — the stone elephant, camel, and qilin are extraordinarily vivid
  • Red walls, stone archways, and ancient camphor trees create a serene, timeless atmosphere
  • Autumn is the peak season: the "Golden Avenue" of ginkgo trees is spectacular
  • The Square City (Sifangcheng) houses a giant stone stele carried by a turtle (bixi)
  • UNESCO World Heritage (2003, as part of "Imperial Tombs of the Ming and Qing Dynasties")

Opening Hours: 7:00 AM – 6:00 PM (Mar–Oct); 7:00 AM – 5:30 PM (Nov–Feb)

Tickets: ¥70 (peak season), ¥60 (off-season)

Transportation: Metro Line 2 to Muxuyuan Station (Exit 3), then walk through the scenic Plum Blossom Hill

Best Duration: 2–3 hours

Best Season: Autumn (late October–November) for golden ginkgo; early spring for plum blossoms

Tips: Combine with Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum (they're adjacent). The Sacred Way is most photogenic in early morning light.

Confucius Temple & Qinhuai River Scenic Area

Location: Gongyuan Street, Qinhuai District

Historical Background: The Qinhuai River has been Nanjing's cultural heart since the Six Dynasties period (3rd–6th centuries). This is where scholars gathered for imperial exams, poets wrote verses by moonlight, and merchants traded silk and tea. Wuyi Lane (Wuyi Xiang), immortalized in Tang Dynasty poetry, still retains its ancient flagstones. The Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao), built in 1034, was the most important examination center in imperial China.

Highlights:

  • Night cruise on Qinhuai River — colored lanterns reflect on the water, recreating the "oar sounds and lantern shadows" of ancient Nanjing
  • Wuyi Lane — walk the same flagstone path that inspired Tang Dynasty poets
  • Jiangnan Examination Hall — the largest imperial examination site in ancient China
  • Zhuque Bridge — breezy evenings here feel like stepping into a classical painting
  • Qinhuai Lantern Festival (during Chinese New Year) — thousands of lanterns illuminate the river

Opening Hours: Temple area 9:00 AM – 10:00 PM; boat rides 6:00 PM – 9:30 PM

Tickets: Confucius Temple ¥30; boat ride ¥80 (night), ¥60 (day); Wuyi Lane free

Transportation: Metro Line 3 to Fuzimiao Station (Exit 2), 5-minute walk

Best Duration: 2–4 hours (ideally late afternoon through evening)

Best Season: Spring Festival for lanterns; spring and autumn evenings for atmosphere

Tips: The area transforms at night — daytime is crowded with tour groups, nighttime is when the real magic happens. Try the street food along the riverbank.

Laomendong (Old Gate East)

Location: East of Zhonghua Gate, Qinhuai District

Historical Background: Laomendong is the best-preserved historic residential district in Nanjing, dating back to the Ming Dynasty. The area sits just east of Zhonghua Gate (the largest castle-style city gate in China), and its narrow flagstone alleys and traditional courtyard houses (siheyuan) have sheltered generations of Nanjing families. In recent years, careful restoration has transformed it into a cultural quarter where centuries-old architecture meets artisan workshops, boutique tea houses, and independent bookstores.

Highlights:

  • Wander the maze of flagstone lanes — every corner reveals a photogenic courtyard or ancient well
  • Artisan shops: paper cutting, velvet flower making, and traditional woodblock printing
  • Rooftop cafes with views of the old city wall and Zhonghua Gate
  • Evening atmosphere: lanterns are lit, and the old bricks glow in warm light
  • Nanjing Impressions (Nanjing Yinxiang) restaurant — authentic local cuisine in a courtyard setting

Opening Hours: Open all day (shops typically 10:00 AM – 9:00 PM)

Tickets: Free (individual shops and workshops charge for products/experiences)

Transportation: Metro Line 3 to Wudingmen Station (Exit 4), 10-minute walk

Best Duration: 2–3 hours

Best Season: All year; evenings are most atmospheric

Tips: Come in late afternoon and stay through sunset — the transition from daylight to lantern-lit evening is magical. Try the osmanthus sugar taro dessert at a local tea house.

Featured Cuisine

Nanjing Salted Duck (盐水鸭 / Yanshui Ya)

Flavor Profile: Tender, succulent, delicately salted with a clean aftertaste. The meat is ivory-white, the skin pale and smooth, with a subtle fragrance of Sichuan peppercorn and star anise. Unlike roast duck, salted duck is served cold — the fat is translucent, and each slice carries a gentle, savory purity.

Historical & Cultural Story: Legend has it that salted duck originated during the Six Dynasties period, perfected over a thousand years. During the Ming Dynasty, it was a tribute dish to the imperial court. Today, it's known as the "First Flavor of Jinling" — a dish so deeply woven into local identity that Nanjing locals eat it weekly, from family dinners to festival feasts. The preparation is deceptively simple: duck is brined, air-dried, then gently poached — the skill lies entirely in timing and seasoning balance.

How to Eat:

  1. Served cold, sliced thin with skin intact
  2. Eat on its own to appreciate the pure duck flavor
  3. Or pair with a light dipping sauce of black vinegar and ginger
  4. Best with warm rice or as a cold appetizer with beer

Price Range: ¥40–80 per whole duck; ¥25–40 per half duck at restaurants; ¥15–20 for a takeaway box

Where to Try:

  • Han Fuxing (韩复兴) — established 1866, the most famous heritage brand
  • Jinling Hotel (金陵饭店) — upscale, perfectly executed version
  • Local wet markets and deli counters — surprisingly excellent and half the price

Duck Blood Vermicelli Soup (鸭血粉丝汤 / Yaxue Fensi Tang)

Flavor Profile: Rich, milky duck bone broth with a deeply savory umami punch. Tender slices of duck blood (with a tofu-like texture), chewy duck gizzard, soft duck liver, and slippery sweet potato vermicelli swim in the golden broth. A sprinkle of cilantro and a dash of chili oil transform each spoonful.

Historical & Cultural Story: This is Nanjing's ultimate comfort food — every local starts their day with a steaming bowl. The dish evolved from the city's millennia-old duck farming tradition: when every part of the duck was used, humble offal became cherished delicacies. Duck blood, in particular, was prized for its silky texture and nutritional value in traditional Chinese medicine. Today, it's the unofficial breakfast of Nanjing — from construction workers to office executives, everyone queues for their morning bowl.

How to Eat:

  1. Slurp the broth first — it's the soul of the dish
  2. Mix in the chili oil and vinegar to taste
  3. Alternate between vermicelli, duck blood, and offal for texture variety
  4. Finish every last drop — the broth at the bottom is the most concentrated

Price Range: ¥15–25 per bowl; ¥10–15 at street stalls

Where to Try:

  • Yin's Soup Dumplings (尹氏汤包) — famous chain, reliable quality
  • Hui Bao Ji (回味) — ubiquitous local chain, open early morning
  • Any street-side breakfast stall near residential neighborhoods

Crab Roe Soup Dumplings (蟹黄汤包 / Xiehuang Tangbao)

Flavor Profile: Paper-thin wrappers encase a golden lake of crab-infused broth. Each dumpling is plump with a mix of fresh crab roe, crab meat, and pork — sweet, briny, and decadently rich. A poke of the chopstick releases an aromatic steam that smells of the Yangtze River.

Historical & Cultural Story: Nanjing sits at the crossroads of Jiangnan's crab country. Every autumn, when hairy crabs from nearby Gaoyou Lake reach peak season, the city's kitchens transform them into these delicate soup dumplings. Unlike the smaller Shanghai xiaolongbao, Nanjing-style crab roe soup dumplings are larger — often served individually in a bamboo steamer with a straw for sipping the broth directly.

How to Eat:

  1. Carefully lift the dumpling by the top knot with chopsticks
  2. Place on your spoon and poke a small hole in the side
  3. Sip the hot broth through the hole (or use a straw)
  4. Add a few drops of black vinegar with shredded ginger
  5. Eat the wrapper and filling in one or two bites

Price Range: ¥25–45 per steamer (6–8 pieces); premium single dumplings ¥15–30 each

Where to Try:

  • Liu Changxing (刘长兴) — century-old brand near Confucius Temple
  • Shi Wang Fu (狮子楼) — famous for their giant single-serving soup dumpling
  • Any restaurant along Qinhuai River during autumn crab season (Sept–Nov)

Folk Culture

Qinhuai Lantern Festival (秦淮灯会)

Origin & History: The Qinhuai Lantern Festival dates back to the Southern Dynasties (420–589 AD), reaching its golden age during the Ming Dynasty when Nanjing was the imperial capital. For over 1,500 years, every Chinese New Year and Lantern Festival (15th day of the first lunar month), the Qinhuai River transforms into a river of light — thousands of handcrafted lanterns in every shape and color are strung along the water, on boats, and throughout the Confucius Temple area. It is recognized as a National Intangible Cultural Heritage.

Performance/Experience Form:

  • Elaborate lantern displays: zodiac animals, mythological creatures, palace scenes
  • Night boat rides through illuminated waterways
  • Lantern riddle guessing (a traditional game)
  • Folk performances: Nanjing Baiju opera, dragon dances, traditional music
  • Street food stalls lining every alleyway

Where to Experience:

  • Confucius Temple (Fuzimiao) main venue
  • Laomendong historic district (satellite displays)
  • Zhonghua Gate (large-scale installations)

Best Time to Experience: Chinese New Year to Lantern Festival (late January–February); lanterns are lit from 6:00 PM to 10:00 PM

Cultural Significance: The lantern festival is Nanjing's most cherished folk tradition — a living connection to the city's imperial past when the capital celebrated with the grandest light displays in China. For locals, visiting the lanterns with family is as essential as Christmas markets are to Europeans.

Nanjing Yunjin Brocade & Intangible Heritage Crafts (南京云锦与非遗手作)

Origin & History: Nanjing is a UNESCO City of Literature and a treasure trove of intangible cultural heritage. Yunjin (cloud brocade), the most famous, has been woven in Nanjing for over 1,600 years — its silk threads, interwoven with gold and peacock feathers, were exclusively for imperial robes. Alongside it, Jinling paper cutting (delicate red paper art), velvet flower making (ronghua), and Jinling Buddhist scripture carving (woodblock printing of Buddhist texts) form a rich tapestry of living traditions passed down through generations.

Performance/Experience Form:

  • Watch Yunjin weavers at work on traditional wooden looms — two people operate one loom, producing only 5–6 cm of brocade per day
  • Try paper cutting — master artisans can create intricate designs in minutes
  • Velvet flower workshops: learn to make the tiny silk flowers that Nanjing brides traditionally wear
  • Nanjing Baiju (南京白局) — a local narrative singing art, soft and melodious, performed in Nanjing dialect

Where to Experience:

  • Nanjing Yunjin Museum (南京云锦博物馆) — live weaving demonstrations
  • Nanjing Intangible Cultural Heritage Museum (南京非遗馆) in Laomendong
  • Ganjia Courtyard (甘熙故居) — a historic mansion with craft workshops

Best Time to Experience: Year-round; museums are open daily; workshops best on weekends

Cultural Significance: These crafts are not just artifacts — they're living proof of Nanjing's role as a cultural capital for 2,600 years. Each thread of Yunjin brocade, each cut of Jinling paper, carries the aesthetic sensibility of generations who found beauty in meticulous craftsmanship.

Travel Tips

Best Time to Visit

Peak Season (Ideal): Spring (March–May) and Autumn (September–November). Spring brings plum blossoms at Plum Blossom Hill (Feb–March) and comfortable temperatures (15–25°C). Autumn paints Purple Mountain in golden ginkgo and red maple — October through November is widely considered Nanjing's most beautiful season.

Shoulder Season: June and early September. Warmer but manageable, fewer tourists, lower hotel rates.

Avoid:

  • July–August: Extremely hot and humid (35–38°C), Nanjing is one of China's "Four Furnaces" in summer
  • Chinese National Day (Oct 1–7): Attractions packed, hotels 2–3x normal price
  • Late January: Can be bitterly cold and damp (though the Lantern Festival is worth braving it)

Recommended Duration

Minimum: 2 full days (Purple Mountain sites + Qinhuai River area)

Ideal: 3 days — add the Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall (profound and sobering), Presidential Palace, and Xuanwu Lake

Extended: 4–5 days — explore Nanjing Museum (one of China's best), Jiming Temple, Qixia Mountain (autumn foliage), and day-trip to nearby Zhenjiang or Yangzhou

Getting Around the City

Metro (Recommended):

  • 11 lines covering most attractions
  • Cost: ¥2–8 per ride
  • Buy single-ride tickets at station machines (English available)
  • Alipay transport QR code works for metro and bus

Taxi/DiDi:

  • Starting fare ¥11 (first 3 km), ¥2.9/km after
  • DiDi app works in English; affordable for most trips within city

Walking/Cycling: The city center is relatively compact. Walking between Confucius Temple, Laomendong, and Zhonghua Gate is pleasant. Shared bikes (Hellobike/Meituan) are everywhere — ¥1.5/30 min.

Intercity Transport

Nanjing Lukou International Airport (NKG): Metro Line S1 connects to the city center (50 min, ¥8). Taxi to city center approximately ¥120–150 (40 min).

High-Speed Rail: Nanjing is a major railway hub.

  • Nanjing South Station (南京南站): Main high-speed rail station
  • To Shanghai: 1–1.5 hours (¥140–230)
  • To Hangzhou: 1.5 hours (¥120–200)
  • To Beijing: 3.5 hours (¥440–750)
  • To Suzhou: 50 minutes (¥100–160)

Practical Tips

Money:

  • Alipay/WeChat Pay accepted everywhere — link your international card before arriving
  • ATMs at Bank of China accept international cards; carry ¥300 backup cash

Language:

  • Mandarin spoken everywhere; younger people may speak basic English in tourist areas
  • Download Pleco (dictionary) or Google Translate offline pack before arriving
  • Have hotel name and address in Chinese on your phone

Internet:

  • Google, WhatsApp, Facebook blocked — use a VPN set up before arrival
  • Free WiFi in most hotels and cafes (passport registration required)
  • China Unicom tourist SIM at airport: ¥100 for 7 days unlimited data

Safety:

  • Nanjing is very safe — low violent crime, streets are lively at night
  • Watch for pickpockets in crowded tourist spots (Fuzimiao, metro during rush hour)
  • Beware of the "teahouse scam": strangers inviting you to a tea ceremony, then presenting an inflated bill

Etiquette:

  • Nanjing locals are known for being warm and straightforward — the city has a relaxed, unhurried vibe
  • No tipping culture; service charges are included
  • Queue politely; Nanjing people generally queue more orderly than in larger cities
  • The Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall (侵华日军南京大屠杀遇难同胞纪念馆) is a solemn site — dress respectfully, speak quietly, no selfies
